Spine Region: Cervical
Type of Imaging: Xray

Measurement: C2 Slope (C2S)

1) Description of Measurement

·           The C2 slope (C2S) is the angle between the inferior endplate of the C2 vertebral body and the horizontal plane on a standing lateral radiograph. It was validated by Protopsaltis et al. (2020) as a simplified, singular marker of cervical deformity that consolidates multiple alignment parameters into one easily obtained measurement.

·           C2 slope serves as a mathematical approximation of T1 slope minus cervical lordosis (T1S–CL) with the aim to simplify the understanding and compensation goals in the cervical spine for primary cervical and primary cervicothoracic deformity.

·           Protopsaltis et al. demonstrated a near-perfect correlation between C2S and T1S–CL in a prospective multicenter cohort of 104 cervical deformity patients: r = 0.97 (p < 0.001) for primary cervical deformity and r = 0.98 (p < 0.001) for cervicothoracic deformity.

·           The principal advantage of C2S is its simplicity and reliability.

·           It requires only a single angular measurement from a landmark (the C2 inferior endplate) that is consistently visible on lateral cervical radiographs (unlike the T1 or C7 endplates, which are frequently obscured by shoulder overlap).

·           C2S is therefore preliminarily useful as an intraoperative alignment guide during cervical deformity correction, where precise measurements of T1 slope and cervical lordosis may be impractical.

·           Intraoperative use: C2S can be measured on intraoperative lateral fluoroscopy prior to final rod assembly. Kim et al. (2025) used a standardized 10° reverse Trendelenburg position (which adds 10° to the measured C2S), targeting an intraoperative C2S of approximately 0° (corresponding to a true standing C2S of ~10°) as the correction goal

2) Instructions to Measure

·           Obtain a standing neutral lateral cervical spine radiograph

·           Identify the inferior endplate of the C2 vertebral body and draw a line along it

·           Draw a horizontal reference line (parallel to the ground)

·           Measure the angle between the inferior endplate of C2 and the horizontal line.

3)        Interpretation:

·           A higher C2 slope indicates that C2 is tilted more anteriorly (forward), suggesting inadequate cervical lordosis relative to thoracic alignment (i.e. greater cervical mismatch)

·           A lower C2 slope indicates a more horizontal C2 endplate with better cervical compensation

3) Normal vs. Pathologic Ranges

•           Estimated normative C2S: approximately 14–17°.

◦           Because C2S approximates T1S–CL, its normative value can be inferred from published T1S–CL data. Iyer et al. (2016) reported a mean T1S–CL of 13.9° in 120 asymptomatic adults; Staub et al. (2018) reported a normative T1S–CL of 16.5° ± 2°. A C2S near 15° has been suggested as consistent with comfortable horizontal gaze given ~30° of normal occiput-to-C2 lordosis.

•           C2S ≥ 17°: corresponds to moderate disability as measured by EQ-5D in the overall cervicothoracic deformity population (Protopsaltis et al. 2020).

•           C2S ≥ 20°: corresponds to moderate disability as measured by NDI in the overall cervicothoracic deformity population (Protopsaltis et al. 2020).

•           C2S ≥ 36°: corresponds to a cSVA ≥ 40 mm (Protopsaltis et al. 2020). (cSVA ≥ 40mm is a widely accepted threshold for cervical deformity with significant associated disability.)

•           C2S correlations with 1-year outcomes (cervicothoracic deformity): Higher postoperative C2S correlated strongly with worse NDI (r = 0.63, p = 0.01) and mJOA (r = −0.65, p = 0.02) at 1-year follow-up. (Protopsaltis et al. 2020)

•           Ideal postoperative C2S < 10°: Passfall et al. (2022) proposed a postoperative C2S < 10° as a realignment target in adult cervical deformity correction. Kim et al. (2025) demonstrated that patients who achieved an intraoperative C2S ≤ 0° (corresponding to ~10° standing) had significantly better final C2S (9.4° vs. 16.9°), greater cervical lordosis, lower T1S–CL, less neck pain, and a higher rate of achieving MCID for JOA recovery (92.6% vs. 66.7%, p = 0.045).

•           In a cervicothoracic deformity cohort of patients, C2S ≥ 36° indicates severe cervical deformity equivalent to a cSVA ≥ 40 mm. (Protopsaltis et al. 2020)

•           Correlation with patient outcomes: Kim et al. (2025) showed final follow-up C2S correlated significantly with neck pain VAS (r = 0.514), NDI (r = 0.380), JOA score (r = −0.301), JOA recovery rate (r = −0.395), EQ-5D index (r = −0.342), and patient-reported improvement rate (r = −0.336), all p < 0.05

Key Principle: C2 slope provides an simple single measurement that captures the mismatch between cervical lordosis and upper thoracic alignment. A postoperative C2S < 10° is the emerging postoperative target, C2S 17–20° corresponds to moderate disability, and C2S ≥ 36° indicates severe cervical deformity equivalent to a cSVA ≥ 40 mm. Emerging targets for C2S in non-deformity populations are still to be determined. Correction goals for patients with less common deformity types such as “flat neck”, are likely warranted. Further studies relating C2 to Cervical Lordosis in other populations are required to substantiate findings further.

Consider evaluating C2 slope in conjunction with T1 slope, C2–7 Cobb angle (cervical lordosis), C2–7 SVA, T1S–CL mismatch, chin-brow vertical angle (CBVA), and thoracic inlet angle (TIA) for comprehensive cervical sagittal alignment assessment.
